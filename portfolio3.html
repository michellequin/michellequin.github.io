Multimodal Interaction

Human Machine Cooperation Group at OFFIS, National Science Foundation (NSF)

As a National Science Foundation (NSF) researcher with Dr. Susanne Boll's Human Machine Cooperation group at the OFFIS Institute for Information Technology in Oldenburg, Germany, I focused on optimizing user safety alerts via multimodal feedback in head-mounted displays. My project contributed to ongoing lab work towrards overcoming alarm and noise desensitization in staff of safety-critical environments, such as hospitals and cargo ships.

What exactly is "multimodal feedback in head-mounted displays"? Essentially, this means personalized and less intrusive alerts or alarms that use multiple sensory types (ex. a phone vibrating AND playing a song for an alarm is multimodal feedback).

I did extensive literature research before designing and implementing a functional prototype that integrated multimodal feedback (vibrotactile and auditory) in head-mounted devices using an Arduino, rendering, 3D printing and Tobii Eye Tracking Glasses. I created and led user experiments, then ran data analysis on the collected 275 responses to determine which feedback patterns were most effective while causing the least user discomfort.

This project contributed to ongoing OFFIS research on overcoming noise desensitization of staff working in noise-laden, safety-critical environments. Examples of target user groups included hospital caregivers monitoring vital patient data or cargo ship captains recognizing collision times of ships. Existing alert mechanisms in such user groups consist of loud, often high-pitched auditory feedback, which cause alarm fatigue that slows user reaction rates and is detrimental to health, safety and productivity. Our aim was to explore multimodal alternatives to traditional auditory alert parameters by exploring a mix of vibrotactile and auditory frequency, rhythmn, tempo patterns, etc.


Expo Poster


For the user study, participants were asked to watch videos while wearing the integrated Tobii Eye Tracker prototype. They were instructed to look up at a target and back down to the video upon recognizing feedback, then fill out a survey after each parameter. 25 parameter patterns were run twice per participant. After running data analysis on the 275 user responses received, we found that the three patterns that caused the most discomfort and/or pain fell under vibro-tactile, while the three that caused the least discomfort fell under auditory.
